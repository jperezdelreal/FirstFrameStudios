# Team Wisdom

Reusable patterns and heuristics learned through work. NOT transcripts — each entry is a distilled, actionable insight. These are the "quotes on the wall" — things every team member should internalize.

## Studio Insights (From Industry Research)

> **"The game tells you what it wants to be if you listen."**
> 
> *— Inspired by Supergiant's development process*
> 
> Playable builds reveal truth faster than any design document. Stop planning and start playing. Every time you ship a build, the game gives you honest feedback. Your job is to listen, not argue.

---

> **"Small team ≠ small ambition."**
> 
> *— Team Cherry's Hollow Knight (3 people, 15M+ copies)*
> 
> We have 12 specialists, not 50. That's not a constraint — it's our advantage. Team size constrains scope, but it doesn't constrain craft. What matters is ruthless clarity about the core loop and everything else that serves it.

---

> **"Kill your darlings, or they'll kill your game."**
> 
> *— From the 155-postmortem meta-analysis: #1 indie failure cause is scope creep*
> 
> A feature that took a week to build but doesn't serve the core loop is a week of waste plus forever of maintenance burden. Cutting a half-built system early saves 100 hours of integration later. Every shipped game is defined by what was cut.

---

> **"Developer joy is a design signal, not a morale problem."**
> 
> *— Swen Vincke (Larian Studios) walked away from guaranteed BG4 revenue because the team wasn't excited*
> 
> If the developers stop being excited about what they're building, that's data. Not a pep-talk issue — a design issue. Measure team excitement like you measure game balance. Low scores mean the design isn't resonating; fix the design, not the mood.

---

> **"Your architecture is invisible to the player; only your output is felt."**
> 
> *— Principle #6: The Player Can't See Architecture*
> 
> Perfect code that stutters is worse than messy code that flows. A technically correct timestep that feels delayed is worse than an "imperfect" timestep that feels responsive. Measure success by what the player's hands feel, not what the code reviews praise.

---

> **"Vision without a keeper is committee-designed."**
> 
> *— Every studio we studied has one creative director or a tiny nucleus (2–3) holding the vision*
> 
> Domain owners execute excellence. But without one person asking "does this feel like *our* game?" across all domains, you get an art style that doesn't match the audio, mechanics that don't match the animation, a game that feels made by five different studios. Vision needs a keeper.

---

> **"Constraints are partners, not enemies."**
> 
> *— Nintendo made Pokémon in 4 shades of green; we made visceral combat in Canvas 2D with no shaders*
> 
> When a desired effect is impossible in your tech stack, don't mourn the limitation — design around it. The result is often more distinctive than the "obvious" solution would have been. Constraints force creative decisions that make your work memorable.

---

> **"Postmortems are how studios compound knowledge into advantage."**
> 
> *— Every studio that ships consistently does formal postmortems after every milestone*
> 
> One project teaching one lesson once is experience. One project teaching one lesson that becomes studio policy for all future projects is compounding knowledge. Document the lesson. Assign an owner. Update the playbook. Or you'll investigate the same question again with the next team.

---

> **"Small teams don't fail because they're too small — they fail because they're overloaded."**
> 
> *— Academic research: overloaded individuals are #1 predictor of indie game crunch*
> 
> The 20% load cap is non-negotiable. If anyone carries more than 20% of a phase's items, stop and redistribute. This is not kindness — it's anti-crunch insurance. Overloaded people make poor decisions, cut corners, and burn out. Distributed work compounds speed.

---

> **"Iteration count correlates with quality; first instincts correlate with overconfidence."**
> 
> *— From Supergiant, Larian, and the 155-postmortem meta-analysis*
> 
> No core mechanic ships after fewer than 3 iteration cycles. If a mechanic feels right on the first try, you haven't tested hard enough. Iteration is not refinement — it's discovery. Each cycle reveals something the previous one missed.
