## 1. Company Name

### Option A: Ironpunch Games

**Rationale:** "Iron" speaks to durability, craft, and the pressure-forged quality of a blacksmith's work. "Punch" speaks to impact — the visceral, immediate sensation of player feel. Together, it's strong, physical, and memorable. It evokes action but also emotional impact ("that scene packed a punch"). Domain-viable: `ironpunchgames.com`.

**Concern:** Leans toward action-game branding. Could feel limiting if we ship a puzzle game or narrative experience. The word "punch" might anchor perception to combat-heavy titles.

### Option B: First Frame Studios

**Rationale:** "First Frame" captures our deepest belief — that the player's experience in the first moment of interaction defines everything. It references animation frames (our craft), the first frame of gameplay (our obsession), and the idea that quality is front-loaded, not back-loaded. It's clean, professional, genre-agnostic, and works in any language. A puzzle game has a first frame. A narrative game has a first frame. An action game has a first frame. Domain-viable: `firstframestudios.com`.

**Strength:** Directly encodes Principle #1 (Player Hands First) into the company name. Tells our philosophy before anyone reads a single document. Works at every scale — from indie to AAA. Sounds professional without being corporate. Sounds creative without being precious.

### Option C: Forgehands Interactive

**Rationale:** A compound word that merges "forge" (craftsmanship under constraints, heat and pressure producing quality) with "hands" (player hands first, handcrafted). Distinctive, unusual, tells a story. Domain-viable: `forgehandsinteractive.com`.

**Concern:** Harder to pronounce internationally. The compound construction feels slightly forced. Less immediately clear than the other two options.

### ✅ Selection: First Frame Studios

**First Frame Studios** is our name. It is the studio's philosophy compressed into two words. Every game we make promises the player something in its first frame — and we spend every subsequent frame delivering on that promise. The name is genre-agnostic, platform-agnostic, IP-agnostic, and internationally clear. It scales from a 12-person squad to a 200-person company without feeling outgrown. It is us.

## 2. Tagline

### **"Forged in Play"**

Three words. Three layers of meaning:

1. **Craftsmanship.** "Forged" — our games are shaped under heat and pressure. We don't assemble; we forge. Constraints are the hammer, iteration is the anvil, and the player experience is the blade.
2. **Player-first process.** "In Play" — we build by playing. Every mechanic is prototyped, playtested, and tuned with hands on the controller before a design document is finalized. The build teaches us. The player teaches us.
3. **Research-driven.** "In Play" — we play the landmark titles before we design. We stand on the shoulders of the genre's solved problems. Our games are forged in the play of every game that came before.

**Full usage:** *First Frame Studios — Forged in Play*

## 3. Studio Description

**First Frame Studios** is a game development company built on a single conviction: the player's experience in the first moment of interaction is a promise, and every system we build exists to keep it. We make games that move players, surprise them, and earn their time — across any genre, any platform, and any IP. Our approach is research-driven, player-first, and relentlessly iterative. We study the landmark titles in our genre before writing a line of code. We ship playable builds fast and let the game teach us what it wants to be. We measure fun with data, not just instinct. And we treat every frame, every input response, every sound effect as a chance to delight someone who trusted us with their time.

We were born from **SimpsonsKong** — a browser-based beat 'em up that started as a passion project and became the crucible that forged our identity. That project taught us everything: that health-cost specials create the best risk/reward loops in the genre, that 0.5x devicePixelRatio can ruin art that's technically correct, that a playable build in 30 minutes teaches more than 30 days of design documents, and that bugs are broken promises to the player. We documented every lesson, every failure, every breakthrough. We built a knowledge base spanning 10 game development disciplines. We wrote leadership principles that govern how we make decisions under pressure. SimpsonsKong isn't our identity — it's our origin story. The lessons it taught us are universal, and we carry them into every project.

What makes us different is how we're built. First Frame Studios operates as a **12-specialist squad** where every domain — game design, engine architecture, gameplay programming, UI/UX, art direction, environment art, character art, VFX, audio design, enemy AI, quality assurance, and production — has a dedicated owner. No one carries two domains. No domain exists in isolation. Our institutional knowledge is documented, searchable, and transferable. We don't lose lessons when people change roles. We don't repeat investigations we've already resolved. We are a studio that doesn't just build games — we build the *capability* to build games. Every project compounds.

## 4. Core DNA

These are the non-negotiable truths that define First Frame Studios. They are not aspirations. They are who we are.

- **The first frame is a promise.** When a player launches our game and touches a button for the first time, we've made them a promise. Every system, every animation, every sound exists to keep it. If the first frame lies, no amount of late-game content redeems it.

- **Constraints forge better games.** We don't mourn limitations — we weaponize them. Nintendo made Pokémon in 4 shades of green. We made combat feel visceral in Canvas 2D with no shaders. Every boundary we accept becomes a creative decision that makes our work more distinctive, not less.

- **Research is the shortcut.** We don't guess what works. We study 35 years of solved problems in our genre, play the games that define it, and design from knowledge — not hope. Originality is earned through mastery, not ignorance of what came before.

- **Ship the playable, then listen.** A running build is the only honest design document. We ship fast, playtest obsessively, measure the results, and iterate. The game tells us what it wants to be. Our job is to listen.

- **Every lesson compounds.** We are not a studio that builds one game and moves on. We are a studio that builds institutional knowledge — documented, searchable, transferable. Every project makes the next one faster, better, and more ambitious. Nothing learned is lost.

## 5. Visual Identity Notes

### Color Palette

The studio palette is separate from any game's palette. It communicates professionalism, creative energy, and technical precision.

| Role | Color | Hex | Rationale |
|------|-------|-----|-----------|
| **Primary** | Deep Midnight Blue | `#0D1B2A` | Authority, depth, professionalism. The "dark mode" of game studios. Signals technical competence without being cold. |
| **Accent** | Ember Orange | `#E85D26` | Energy, warmth, the forge. Represents the heat of creation, the spark of play. Contrasts powerfully against the deep blue. |
| **Secondary** | Cool Steel | `#415A77` | Supporting tone. Technical, calm, trustworthy. Used for body text, secondary UI, and documentation. |
| **Highlight** | Bright Forge Gold | `#F5A623` | Achievement, premium moments, key CTAs. Used sparingly — like gold in a blacksmith's work, it marks the exceptional. |
| **Neutral** | Warm Off-White | `#F0EDE8` | Background, breathing space. Warmer than pure white — avoids clinical feel. Signals approachability alongside the darker tones. |

**Overall feel:** Dark, warm, professional. Like a well-lit forge at midnight — precise work happening with visible passion. Not corporate sterile. Not indie chaotic. *Crafted.*

### Logo Concept Direction

- **Core symbol:** A stylized film/animation frame — a rectangular border with one corner subtly broken or transformed. The "break" represents the moment the static frame becomes alive through player interaction. The frame is precise (craftsmanship), but the break introduces energy (play).
- **Wordmark:** "FIRST FRAME" in a clean, geometric sans-serif. Letterspacing slightly wide for authority. "STUDIOS" smaller, underneath, tracked even wider.
- **Monogram variant:** "FF" ligature for favicon, app icon, and small-format use. The two F's share a vertical stroke — suggesting unity, shared foundation, two frames becoming one.
- **Color application:** Primary mark in Warm Off-White on Deep Midnight Blue. Accent applications use Ember Orange for the frame-break element.
- **What it is NOT:** Not pixelated. Not retro. Not cute. Not a mascot. We are a studio, not a nostalgia brand. The mark should feel like it belongs on a game's splash screen *and* on a business card.

### Typography Direction

| Use | Direction | Rationale |
|-----|-----------|-----------|
| **Headlines / Logo** | Geometric sans-serif (e.g., direction of Eurostile, Rajdhani, or Industry) | Technical precision with personality. Geometric shapes echo the "frame" motif. Wide letterforms suggest confidence. |
| **Body text** | Humanist sans-serif (e.g., direction of Inter, Source Sans, or Nunito) | Readable, warm, approachable. Balances the technical headline font with human warmth. Good screen legibility at all sizes. |
| **Code / Technical docs** | Monospace (e.g., direction of JetBrains Mono or Fira Code) | We are a studio that documents everything. Technical documentation is a first-class artifact. The monospace font signals: this is precise, this is real, this is how we build. |

## 7. Studio Inspirations

These are the real studios that have shaped our thinking. Each one models something we aspire to.

| Studio | Why We Admire Them | The Lesson We Carry |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Supergiant Games** | Singular creative vision (Greg Kasavin) guided every design decision. Small team (20–25), massive ambition. Shipped Hades with uncompromising artistry. | Vision needs a keeper. Small teams don't mean small dreams — they mean ruthless prioritization around one unified voice. Every pixel and sound reflects the same taste. |
| **Team Cherry** | 3 people made Hollow Knight (15M+ copies). They didn't add features for years because they didn't serve the core loop. Silksong's development prioritizes "is it fun?" over schedule. | "Small team ≠ small ambition" and "Kill your darlings" are not sacrifices — they're the keys to creating something memorable. Features that don't strengthen the core are distractions. |
| **Nintendo** | Obsessive playtesting. They discard *months* of work if a prototype isn't fun. Stable leadership (99% employee retention) paired with developer mobility (engineers rotate between franchises, cross-pollinating ideas). | Playtest-driven iteration is not a nice-to-have, it's the foundation. Studio culture should reward discarding bad work as much as shipping good work. Stability + movement = growth. |
| **Sandfall Interactive** | After Expeditions: Rome sold millions, they publicly committed to staying at 30–33 people forever. This is intentional: small teams move faster, communicate clearer, and maintain creative coherence. They outsource execution (QA, localization, marketing) but keep vision inside. | Intentional smallness is a competitive advantage. Know which work is "core vision" (must stay inside) vs. "execution support" (outsource without guilt). |
| **ConcernedApe (Stardew Valley)** | One person. 4.5 years in a basement. Shipped a timeless game because one person's vision never got diluted by committee votes, budget pressure, or publisher mandates. | Vision without compromise creates games people love for decades. Solo development isn't the path for everyone, but the *purity of vision* is non-negotiable regardless of team size. |
| **Larian Studios** | Swen Vincke walked away from Baldur's Gate 4 because the team wasn't excited. The 155-postmortem meta-analysis confirms: team morale is a top-5 success factor. BG3's success came from a studio that chose developer joy over guaranteed revenue. | Developer joy is a *signal*, not a morale problem. If the team isn't excited about what they're building, the game won't be either. Measure team excitement like you measure game balance. |

*We don't aspire to copy these studios. We aspire to embody the principles they've proven: vision coherence, ruthless triage, playtest discipline, intentional scale, creative purity, and team morale as a leading indicator of game quality.*

## 8. Genre Strategy — Vertical Growth

### Philosophy

We are a **game studio**, not a genre studio. First Frame Studios exists to make great games — period. Each project we undertake deepens our expertise in a new genre vertical. Beat 'em up is our entry point, not our ceiling. The name "First Frame" was chosen precisely because it applies to *every* genre: a platformer has a first frame, a fighting game has a first frame, an RPG has a first frame.

### Vertical Model

Each genre we tackle becomes a **vertical** — a dedicated body of skills, patterns, research, and institutional knowledge specific to that genre. When we master a vertical, we own it permanently. Verticals are organized as skills in the repository: `skills/{genre}-{topic}/SKILL.md`. A vertical is not a single skill file — it's a *collection* of skills that together represent deep competence in a genre.

### Current Verticals

- ✅ **Beat 'Em Up** (active) — Our first vertical, informed by 9 reference games (Streets of Rage 2–4, TMNT: Shredder's Revenge, Castle Crashers, Scott Pilgrim, Final Fight, River City Girls, Dragon's Crown), with dedicated skills for combat system design, enemy AI, 2.5D movement, pacing, and game feel. This vertical proved our process: genre study → GDD → backlog → build → retrospective → skills capture.

### Future Verticals (aspirational, not committed)

These represent genres we *could* pursue. They are directions, not promises. Each would receive the same depth of research and skill-building we gave beat 'em ups.

- 🎯 **Platformer** — movement physics, level design grammar, precision controls, momentum systems, coyote time, jump buffering
- 🎯 **Fighting Game** — frame-perfect inputs, netcode (rollback vs delay), character balance, hitbox visualization, training mode design
- 🎯 **Action RPG** — progression systems, loot tables, skill trees, procedural content generation, economy balancing, build diversity
- 🎯 **Puzzle** — mechanic introduction curves, "aha moment" design, difficulty ramps without frustration, minimalist tutorialization
- 🎯 **3D Action** — camera management, spatial combat, environment navigation, lock-on systems, depth perception feedback

### How Verticals Grow

When we start a new genre project, we research it with the same rigor we applied to beat 'em ups. The process is always the same:

1. **Genre study** — Play and analyze the landmark titles. Document what works, what doesn't, and why.
2. **GDD** — Write a game design document informed by research, not guesswork.
3. **Backlog** — Break the GDD into buildable work items.
4. **Build** — Ship playable builds fast. Let the game teach us.
5. **Retrospective** — Capture what we learned about the genre and about ourselves.
6. **Skills capture** — Document genre-specific patterns as permanent, reusable skills.

Each vertical becomes **permanent knowledge**. We never lose what we learn. A vertical completed for one project accelerates every future project in that genre.

### Cross-Vertical Transfer

Many skills transfer between genres and compound across verticals:

- **Transferable:** State machines, audio design, UI/UX patterns, QA methodology, project conventions, multi-agent coordination, input handling, game feel principles, animation systems, performance optimization
- **Genre-specific (additive):** Combo systems (beat 'em up), wall-jumping physics (platformer), rollback netcode (fighting), loot generation (RPG), puzzle logic solvers (puzzle), camera orbiting (3D action)

Genre-specific skills don't replace transferable ones — they're *additive*. Every new vertical makes the studio stronger in its core capabilities while adding a new dimension of genre expertise.

### Team Adaptability

The squad's roles — Lead, Engine, Gameplay, UI, Art, Sound, QA, Game Designer, and the rest — are **genre-agnostic**. Yoda is the Game Designer whether we're making a beat 'em up or a puzzle game. Chewie is the Engine Engineer whether we're in Canvas 2D or Godot 4 or Unreal. What changes per genre is the **skills they read**, not their charters. The squad structure is the constant; the genre knowledge is the variable.

This is by design. A studio that must restructure its team for every genre is fragile. A studio whose team structure *absorbs* new genres is antifragile. First Frame Studios is built to be the latter.
